Ray tracing and rasterization.

What is that, what do you eat it with?

[This is an article and in no way an informative text]

First, to make sure the reader can understand this text, in this article the explanation is of “real-time rendered ray tracing”, not usual ray tracing, as any GPU is capable of the latter.

One of the things that has taken the graphical industry by surprise was the real-time ray tracing capabilities of certain Graphic processing units (GPU’s), however, this technology is still quite immature and expensive, not allowing everyone to have access to such “privilege”, and in sight of this problem, which has actually existed for some decades already, many technologies have been created in order to “cheat” the high performance requirements that this “path tracing” needs in order to be useful.

This technology is known as rasterization and it has become the standard for real time rendering in the entire industry, this is due to it’s usually low performance impact compared to path tracing as rasterization follows a different set of rules, for example: Instead of rendering each single shadow dynamically on a scene, a “shadow map” is created prior to the scene rendering, whereas only shadows in moving objects are rendered dynamically, and even then, a different type of shadow map is applied to lower the load on the GPU, this is also applied to reflection maps. This all differs greatly from traditional path tracing, and you may have noticed the use of both “path” and “ray” tracing in this article, at their core, they are the same thing, except path tracing only calculates light bounces, this is the way this type of rendering creates shadows, reflections and others, that enter the scene camera, which may create “artifacts” (errors in scene rendering) but requires way less computing power to achieve similar results to ray tracing.

In the end, these two technologies will keep on advancing toe-to-toe because the extra performance overhead given by rasterization will always be taken with open arms, as well as the high fidelity lighting that path tracing is able to give us, only time will decide if rasterization will stay at the top, or if it will fall as ray tracing becomes more accessible to everyone.
